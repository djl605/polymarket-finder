3. **Market efficiency analysis**

A charitable interpretation of the **77.0%** price is: traders know Lululemon often beats its own guidance, know holiday performance tracked to the high end, and believe final EPS could come in a few cents above the prior range because of conservative management assumptions, buybacks, or tax/share-count effects. Given the company’s recent streak of upside surprises, that is not a crazy view. ([corporate.lululemon.com](https://corporate.lululemon.com/media/press-releases/2025/06-05-2025-210525682))

But there are reasons to doubt that 77% is a very efficient number:
- **Low volume** means this could easily reflect a small number of traders extrapolating “LULU usually beats” without fully noticing the more important detail: **the strike is above management’s own updated range**.
- The spread is tight, but in thin markets a tight spread does **not** guarantee information quality; it may only mean there is little disagreement among a few participants.
- This outcome does not look “obvious.” If it were obvious, one would want stronger evidence than “high end of guidance” and “they often beat.” Here, the hard evidence still centers the quarter **below** the strike. That makes a 77% yes price look more like an aggressive extrapolation than a settled consensus.

4. **Mispricing assessment**

My best read is that there is **some credible evidence of overpricing on YES**, but not a gigantic mispricing. The decisive fact is that the company’s last official Q4 guide was **$4.66-$4.76**, and its later January update only pointed to the **high end** of that range, not above it. Meanwhile, the market requires **more than $4.78**. That means traders buying YES at 77% are implicitly assuming either:
1. management is again materially sandbagging, and/or  
2. unofficial consensus drift above the company’s range is more informative than management’s latest commentary.

That can happen, but the public evidence I found does **not** clearly support a probability as high as 77%. At the same time, I would be cautious about claiming a huge edge, because Lululemon has a real history of beating guidance and holiday commentary did improve late in the quarter. The most plausible fair value from the public evidence looks closer to a **coin flip to modest favorite for NO**, or at least something materially below 77% YES.

So: **yes, there is credible evidence of mild-to-moderate mispricing, with YES looking too expensive**. I do **not** see evidence for an extreme >20 cent edge after applying the “challenge your reasoning” check, because repeated upside surprises and the January holiday update are exactly the kinds of things informed traders could be pricing.
