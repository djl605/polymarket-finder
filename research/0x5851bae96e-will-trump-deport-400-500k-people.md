Reaching 400k+ in FY2026 would require a ~50%+ increase relative to that FY2024 ICE figure. ([ice.gov](https://www.ice.gov/news/releases/ice-releases-fiscal-year-2024-annual-report?utm_source=openai))

This shows 400–500k is not totally outside historical DHS totals but would be high for ICE alone in recent decades. ([ice.gov](https://www.ice.gov/features/ERO-2019?utm_source=openai))

B. Evidence that could make 400–500k in FY2026 plausible (bullish for YES)
- Substantial ramp-up of interior enforcement in 2025–early‑2026: independent trackers and NGO monitors documented sharp increases in ICE interior operations beginning after the Jan 2025 inauguration — more arrests, elevated detention counts, more charter/commercial removal flights (Human Rights First’s ICE Flight Monitor). ([humanrightsfirst.org](https://humanrightsfirst.org/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/January-2026-ICE-Flight-Monitor-Report-.pdf?utm_source=openai))
- Detention population and detention capacity grew through late 2025 (TRAC and other trackers reported detention counts in the tens of thousands and notable increases in booked arrests in interior operations). Higher detention counts and more domestic transfer/removal flights are necessary prerequisites for raising removals. If those operational increases were sustained and scaled nationwide across FY2026, ICE removals could increase substantially. ([factually.co](https://factually.co/fact-checks/justice/independent-datasets-trac-academic-studies-ice-interior-arrests-deportations-2025-2026-b83cfd?utm_source=openai))
- Large, concentrated enforcement operations (e.g., Operation “Metro Surge” in Minnesota and similar interior efforts) demonstrate the administration’s willingness to mount mass interior enforcement campaigns that can produce thousands of arrests/removals in short periods; multiplying those campaigns could materially increase annual removals if replicated at scale and sustained. Reporting shows the administration executed large operations and increased flights and transfers in 2025–early 2026. ([washingtonpost.com](https://www.washingtonpost.com/national/2026/02/13/minnesota-metro-surge-ice/757485de-0906-11f1-b196-5e1986b3575c_story.html?utm_source=openai))

C. Evidence that argues 400–500k is unlikely (bearish for YES)
- ICE’s FY2024 baseline (271k) and independent tallies for 2025/early‑2026: independent analysts (TRAC, Deportation Data Project, Migration Policy and other trackers) that reconstruct ICE’s posted removals show totals for FY2025 and early FY2026 that, when combined, are well below an extra ~130k–230k annual increase needed to hit 400–500k in a single FY. TRAC’s aggregations and other independent reconstructions place total ICE removals during the early Trump return period in the low‑to‑mid hundreds of thousands (not near 400–500k for a single FY). That is concrete evidence that, through the first part of the administration, ICE removals have not exploded to the required scale. ([tracreports.org](https://tracreports.org/reports/767/?utm_source=openai))
- Reporting and fact‑checks indicate public administration claims of “hundreds of thousands” sometimes aggregate CBP expulsions, voluntary departures, and other DHS counts that are distinct from ICE ERO removals. Because the market resolves on ICE’s ERO removals metric specifically, broader DHS headline totals are not directly relevant unless they appear in the ICE FY2026 annual report. Many high headline totals are driven by CBP expulsions and other categories not counted the same way by ICE. Traders who conflate these definitions could misprice the market, but responsible arbitrage would be limited if they already understand the resolution metric. ([factually.co](https://factually.co/fact-checks/politics/how-many-deportations-2025-2026-93613e?utm_source=openai))
- Logistical and international constraints: substantially higher removals require more flight capacity, detention beds, and cooperation from foreign governments to accept repatriations; diplomatic and legal limits, judicial challenges, and state‑level resistance (litigation over operations such as the Minnesota surge) can slow, limit, or reverse enforcement intensity. Multiple news outlets document legal pushback and disruptions that can lower throughput. Even with aggressive policy, sustaining a 50%+ net increase for a full fiscal year is operationally nontrivial. ([washingtonpost.com](https://www.washingtonpost.com/politics/2026/02/06/minnesota-immigration-crackdown-court-strain-contempt/?utm_source=openai))

Given the revision risk, headline figures early in FY2026 may be updated later — an uncertainty traders will price in.
