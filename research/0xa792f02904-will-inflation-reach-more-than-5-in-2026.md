3) MARKET EFFICIENCY ANALYSIS — interpreting the market price (17.5%)
- Reasonable-charitable interpretation(s) of 17.5%: the price can be seen as a tail‑risk premium rather than a central‑case probability. Because a single month above 5% is all that’s needed, an event that causes a very sharp but short headline spike (large energy shock, immediate tariff pass‑through, or an unusually large one‑month revision or data anomaly) could make a >5% print possible, so traders may be attributing a non‑negligible chance to extreme but plausible shocks. EIA‑flagged oil war/Strait‑of‑Hormuz scenarios are an example of an event that could produce a temporary headline spike. ([eia.gov](https://www.eia.gov/pressroom/releases/press584.php?utm_source=openai))
- Could the price reflect one/few overconfident trader(s)? Yes — total trading volume is low ($4,871), age 121 days, and the spread is tight (3¢). Low‑volume + tight spread often means the displayed price can be held by one or a few active accounts; it does not reliably indicate broad aggregation of diverse information. That raises the possibility this is a concentrated view rather than broad consensus.
- Is low volume explained by “obvious” outcome? No: mainstream data and forecasters point to disinflation in 2026 (median CPI/PCE forecasts ~2–3%), which would make YES unlikely. If the outcome were obvious (NO), the market would likely sit near 0% and still attract no trades. The fact the market sits at 17.5% suggests either (a) traders assigned material tail risk, or (b) price is set by a small number of traders who value the tail differently. Consensus forecasts (Fed, SPF, OECD) suggest the market is more likely overpricing the tail than underpricing it. ([federalreserve.gov](https://www.federalreserve.gov/monetarypolicy/fomcprojtabl20251210.htm?utm_source=openai))

4) MISPRICING ASSESSMENT — weighing evidence and the critical check
- What would have to happen for a >5% monthly YoY CPI print? An abrupt, large rise in headline CPI components (notably energy and food) plus sustained or very rapid re‑acceleration in shelter or services. Given weights (energy ≈ 6.2%, shelter ≈ 35%), energy alone would need to more than double relative to the same month in 2025 or be accompanied by large jumps across many categories to lift the 12‑month headline above 5%. ([bls.gov](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/archives/cpi_03112026.htm?utm_source=openai))

  - Have I misunderstood resolution? No — the market resolves to YES if any BLS monthly report in 2026 has the 12‑month CPI > 5.0% (BLS reports to one decimal). I used BLS releases as the authoritative resolution source. ([bls.gov](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/archives/cpi_03112026.htm?utm_source=openai))
  - Could traders have access to nonpublic information that makes >5% materially more likely? Not that I found in credible public reporting; the major drivers that could cause >5% (large energy supply cutoff, massive tariff pass‑through, or a data/revision anomaly) are either publicly visible risks (geopolitics/tariffs) or extremely large shocks that forecasters would also be tracking. It’s plausible a few traders are simply betting tail scenarios; that does not prove the market price is correct.
- Overall mispricing judgement:
  - Based on authoritative data and consensus forecasts, there is credible evidence that the true probability of any 2026 month’s headline CPI YoY exceeding 5% is substantially lower than 17.5% — I estimate a best central estimate for the true probability in the low single digits (roughly 2–5%), dominated by tail scenario risk (sustained energy shock or extreme tariff passthrough). That said, the existence of plausible (if low‑probability) shocks makes the market’s 17.5% not absurd as a tail premium. I therefore assess a mispricing exists (market too high), but it’s not an obvious arbitrage — the difference is a moderate tail‑premium vs consensus.
